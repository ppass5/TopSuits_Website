Welcome

Have you ever played a video game that didn't display your score? Or show you what type of tools you had to use? How confusing would that be! Topsuits is a team of University students that are developing and designing for the NASA Spacesuit User Interface Technologies for Students (NASA S.U.I.T.S) design challenge. The team created a spacesuit visual display using the augmented reality (AR) Microsoft HoloLens. The visual display, or informatics as they call it, is to aid astronauts while outside of the spaceship performing maintenance or other activites. Just like the Pokémon on Pokémon go or the character bubble on Roblox, it guides the user to where they can find all the correct information! 

Team

The team is made up of members from the University of Miami and University of North Florida. From backgrounds in biology to computer science, this electic team works together to achieve one end goal: Making cool stuff for space!

WHAT IS AUGMENTED REALITY?

The team is using a headset known as the Microsoft Hololens. Just like Iron Man's heads up display (HUD), when the user puts on the headset to play a game or conduct a spacewalk, the lens overlays graphics onto the real world space.

AR VS VR

While using the hololens to design for AR, the team uses four different types of interactions for the interface.

In virtual reality you look into the world of the device, like watching a movie and are largely used with headset. Augmented reality is different in that the assets are brought into your world. It can be on anything from an iPad, to phone to the Hololens which the team is using to develop.

Designing for a space is different from what we currently do, which is design for screens. So in this environment we're placing elements in the users space so that can become confusing once they start manipulating those elements. Because on a screen they can either click, hover, play something or scroll through the screen. In augmented reality they are able to do much more with those elements.

Morgan Mckie, Designer

TYPE OF INTERACTIONS

While using the hololens to design for AR, the team uses four different types of interactions for the interface.

GAZE

The position is used as a cursor, look in different directions the small gaze icon will follow.

BLOOM

For the hololens, this brings up the main menu. It's very useful as it can be used at any time.

VOICE

Voice commands can be used to bring up menus or change displays. Very useful for astronauts who might have their hands full or less dexterity with their gloves.

PINCH

Used to start an app or move a box, this is very versatile gesture.

NASA AND AUGMENTED REALITY

In 2016 astronaut Scott Kelly (International Space Station) received Sidekick, a NASA developed augmented reality device. Sidekick provides hands-free, heads-up assistance to crew members in space, enabling new ways to communicate with each other and with support personnel on Earth. High-definition holograms integrate with physical places and objects, allowing crew members to see schematics or diagrams in 3-D to help them complete tasks. Sidekick operates in two modes, allowing it to work as a simple reference tool or as a teleconferencing tool. When asked about his time with Sidekick, astronaut Scott Kelly said "...I immediately noticed this is something we can use right now."

Space systems rely on cutting-edge technology...we are dealing with a system that needs to be autonomous. You're going to be far away from earth, your communication time...is going to be this huge delay. There is not going to be this real-time interface, so you have to have something that can allow you make decisions on your own when you are far away from other people...we are trying to leverage these really smart computing systems that are small and travel with you so you can implement those anywhere

Brian Hicks, Developer
